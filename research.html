Pushing the Boundary of Quantum Advantage in Hard Combinatorial Optimization

Nature Communications, 2025

This work demonstrates that probabilistic computers, co-designed with hardware to implement Monte Carlo algorithms, provide a scalable classical pathway for solving hard optimization problems. We focus on three-dimensional spin glasses and benchmark against leading quantum annealers. Our adaptive parallel tempering algorithm, supported by non-local isoenergetic cluster moves, scales favorably and establishes a rigorous classical baseline for assessing practical quantum advantage. Field Programmable Gate Arrays can implement these algorithms in modern hardware, leveraging massive parallelism to accelerate them while improving energy efficiency.

All-to-All Reconfigurability with Sparse and Higher-Order Ising Machines

Nature Communications, 2024

We evaluate probabilistic bit-based Ising Machines on 3-regular 3-Exclusive OR Satisfiability as a representative hard optimization problem. We introduce a multiplexed architecture that emulates all-to-all network functionality while maintaining highly parallelized chromatic Gibbs sampling. Our FPGA implementation demonstrates competitive algorithmic and prefactor advantages over alternative Ising machines. We also implement higher-order interactions that lead to better prefactors without changing algorithmic scaling. Scaled magnetic versions of p-bit Ising machines could lead to orders of magnitude improvements over the state of the art for generic optimization.

Training Deep Boltzmann Networks with Sparse Ising Machines

Nature Electronics, 2024

We demonstrate a new application for probabilistic bit-based Ising machines by training deep generative AI models with them. Using sparse, asynchronous, and massively parallel Ising machines, we train deep Boltzmann networks in a hybrid probabilistic-classical computing setup. For MNIST, our machine using only 4,264 nodes achieves the same classification accuracy as an optimized software-based restricted Boltzmann Machine with approximately 3.25 million parameters. Our hybrid computer takes a measured 50 to 64 billion probabilistic flips per second, at least an order of magnitude faster than GPU/TPU implementations.

Accelerated Quantum Monte Carlo with Probabilistic Computers

Communications Physics, 2023

We demonstrate 2 to 3 orders of magnitude acceleration of a standard quantum Monte Carlo algorithm using a specially designed digital processor, and a further 2 to 3 orders of magnitude by mapping it to a clockless analog processor. Our demonstration provides a roadmap for 5 to 6 orders of magnitude acceleration for a transverse field Ising model and could be extended to other QMC models. The clockless analog hardware provides performance within a factor of less than 10 of quantum annealers while operating at room temperature with classical devices.

A Full-Stack View of Probabilistic Computing with p-bits

IEEE JXCDC, 2023

We provide a comprehensive full-stack review of probabilistic computing with p-bits as a representative example of energy-efficient and domain-specific computing. From hardware, architecture, and algorithmic perspectives, we outline the main applications of probabilistic computers ranging from probabilistic machine learning and AI to combinatorial optimization and quantum simulation. We argue that p-bits could be used to build energy-efficient probabilistic systems tailored for probabilistic algorithms and applications, with orders of magnitude improvements in energy efficiency compared to conventional approaches.

Emulating Quantum Circuits with Generalized Ising Machines

IEEE Access, 2023

We present an exact and general procedure for mapping any sequence of quantum gates onto a network of probabilistic p-bits. Each configuration represents a Feynman path with a complex amplitude that can be associated with a complex energy. The real part generates samples of Feynman paths, while the imaginary part is accounted for by treating samples as complex entities. By introducing a real part in the energy function through suitable transformations, we can harness powerful sampling algorithms like Gibbs sampling to achieve acceptable results with fewer samples, potentially escaping exponential scaling.
